## 4-Layer — PCR Products Purification

Designed for gel recovery and cleanup of PCR amplification products. The 4-layer silica membrane efficiently captures DNA fragments (typically 100 bp–10 kb) from agarose gel slices or PCR reaction mixtures while removing unincorporated primers, dNTPs, enzymes, and buffer salts.

**Applications:** Gel extraction, PCR cleanup, enzymatic reaction cleanup, labeling reaction purification, adapter ligation cleanup for NGS library prep.

---

## 6-Layer — Plasmid DNA Miniprep

Higher binding capacity for plasmid DNA from bacterial cultures (1–5 ml overnight E. coli). The 6-layer design provides sufficient binding surface for up to 20 μg of plasmid DNA per column with A260/A280 ratios of 1.8–2.0.

**Applications:** Bacterial plasmid miniprep, cloning vector purification, plasmid DNA for transformation and transfection.

**⚠️ Overloading note:** Processing more than 5 mL of high-copy-number culture on a single column causes genomic DNA contamination. Split across multiple columns for volumes above 5 mL.

## 8-Layer — Genomic DNA and Total RNA Extraction

The thickest membrane configuration for maximum binding capacity. Designed for genomic DNA and total RNA extraction from complex biological samples including cultured cells, animal tissues, plant tissues, blood, and bacteria. 

Compatible with common chaotropic salt-based binding buffers (guanidine hydrochloride, guanidine thiocyanate). Recommended for challenging or low-yield samples.

**Applications:** gDNA from cells/tissues/blood, total RNA extraction, viral DNA/RNA from serum/plasma.

## Binding Chemistry

Silica-mediated nucleic acid binding operates via chaotropic salt-driven dehydration:

1. **Binding:** 4–6 M GuHCl or GuSCN strips the hydration shell from DNA phosphate backbone and silica surface. High ionic strength screens electrostatic repulsion. DNA binds to silica via hydrogen bonds (phosphate oxygen ↔ silanol Si–OH) + van der Waals interactions. Binding constant Kₐ ≈ 10⁶–10⁷ M⁻¹, primarily entropy-driven (release of ordered water).

2. **Washing:** 70–80% ethanol wash buffer removes salts and proteins while maintaining the dehydrated DNA-silica complex.

3. **Elution:** Low-ionic-strength buffer (TE, pH 8.0–8.5, or nuclease-free water) rehydrates both surfaces, competitively displacing DNA. Heating to 55–70 °C improves yield by 10–30%.

---

## Protocol Optimization Tips

- **Binding buffer pH:** Optimal range 5.0–6.5. Below 5.0 → depurination risk. Above 6.5 → reduced binding from increased silanol deprotonation.
- **Chaotrope concentration:** Final concentration must exceed ~3 M. Standard 5:1 buffer:sample ratio for PCR cleanup.
- **Dry spin:** 2–3 min at max speed after final wash eliminates residual ethanol that inhibits Taq polymerase and T4 DNA ligase.
- **Two-step elution:** Split elution volume into two aliquots for 10–20% higher total recovery.
- **Fragment cutoff:** Recovery drops sharply below 100 bp. For fragments <200 bp, add 1 volume isopropanol to binding mix.

## Troubleshooting

| Symptom | Cause | Fix |
|---|---|---|
| Low yield (<50%) | Insufficient chaotrope | Verify buffer:sample ratio; check pH |
| Low A260/A230 (<1.8) | Ethanol/chaotrope carryover | Extra dry spin 2–3 min |
| Low A260/A280 (<1.7) | Protein contamination | Reduce sample input; extra wash |
| High A260/A280 (>2.2) | RNA contamination | Add RNase A during lysis |
| Column clogs | Incomplete agarose dissolution | Ensure full melting at 50–60 °C |
| Failed downstream PCR | Residual ethanol | Air-dry column 5 min before elution |
